\textbf{\Large ABSTRACT}
\vspace{0.5cm}
\hrule
\vspace{0.5cm}

This thesis designs, implements, and evaluates a hybrid LLM-augmented shopping recommender with grounded output. The work is motivated by the failure modes documented in the largest production AI shopping assistant: Amazon's Rufus is credited with roughly \$12 billion of incremental revenue in 2025, yet independent investigations report 32\% recommendation accuracy, 28\% price hallucination, 83\% self-serving recommendations, and routine fabrication of review themes. The thesis argues that these failures are not intrinsic to the AI-shopping problem but follow from specific integration choices, and that a different system can make those choices differently.

The prototype combines three structural decisions: a reactive-first interaction model where behavior signals prepare context but the user always initiates an LLM call, a two-stage recommendation pipeline where classical algorithms generate candidates and the LLM only re-ranks and explains, and a hydration-over-generation principle where the LLM emits product identifiers, the client renders live catalog data, and an output-validation layer verifies every cited identifier, strips factual claims from free text, and checks review themes against the source corpus.

A custom evaluation harness scores grounded quality, latency, and cost across an eight-configuration sweep, combining deterministic shopping-domain scorers with cross-family LLM-judge rubrics. The harness produces a pareto front on cost-quality and latency-quality plus a composite ranking that defends the shipped configuration on measured evidence; the best-composite cell costs \$0.0003 per query at 4.2~s p95 latency, with grounded output across the entire sweep.
